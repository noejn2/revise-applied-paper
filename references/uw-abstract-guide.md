# Writing an abstract — UW–Madison Writing Center

Distilled from "Writing an Abstract for Your Research Paper," The Writing Center,
University of Wisconsin–Madison:
<https://writing.wisc.edu/handbook/assignments/writing-an-abstract-for-your-research-paper/>

Complements §8.3 of `bellemare-checklist.md`: Bellemare says *how to derive* the abstract
(from the introduction's hook, research question, and value-added) and sets the intelligibility
bar; this guide adds the structural criteria below.

## Purpose

A good abstract lets a reader get the gist of the paper quickly and decide whether to read it,
prepares them to follow the full argument, helps them remember the key points later, and gives
search engines and bibliographic databases the terms they index the paper under. Many readers —
and citers — never go past it.

## Length and format

- One paragraph, roughly 6–7 sentences, **150–250 words**.
- Defer to the target journal's stated limit or required structure (some journals use
  structured abstracts with fixed headings and word caps).

## Content elements, in typical order

1. **Context / topic** — the background and the specific topic under study.
2. **Research question / problem** — the central question the research addresses.
3. **What's already known** — briefly, what previous research has shown (often implied
   rather than stated).
4. **Rationale / value-added** — why this research, what it contributes.
5. **Methods and data** — the research and analytical methods used.
6. **Main findings** — the results or arguments, concretely.
7. **Significance** — the implications of the findings.

The proportion of space given to each element varies by discipline and genre; some elements
may be implied. But missing findings or a missing question is a defect, not a style choice.

## Rules

- **Standalone:** intelligible on its own — the reader must not need the paper to follow it.
- **No citations:** abstracts summarize what *you* studied and found, not the literature.
- **Write it last:** draft or finalize the abstract after the full paper exists, so you know
  what you are summarizing. (Compatible with Bellemare's advice to assemble it from the
  introduction's first sentences.)
- **Tense (social-science convention):** present tense for current interpretations, methods,
  and findings; past tense for previous research. (Matches Bellemare's present-tense norm for
  data and results.)
- **Discoverability:** include the key terms a target reader would search for — abstracts and
  titles drive indexing.
